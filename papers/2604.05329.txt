Abstract
Generative Recommendation (GR) has recently transitioned from
atomic item-indexing to Semantic ID (SID)-based frameworks to
capture intrinsic item relationships and enhance generalization.
However, the adoption of high-granularity SIDs leads to two critical
challenges: prohibitive training overhead due to sequence expan-
sion and unstable performance reliability characterized by non-
monotonic accuracy fluctuations. We identify that these disparate
issues are fundamentally rooted in the Semantic Dilution Effect,
where redundant tokens waste massive computation and dilute the
already sparse learning signals in recommendation. To counteract
this, we propose STAMP (Semantic Trimming and Auxiliary Multi-
step Prediction), a framework utilizing a dual-end optimization
strategy. We argue that effective SID learning requires simultane-
ously addressing low input information density and sparse output
supervision. On the input side, Semantic Adaptive Pruning (SAP)
dynamically filters redundancy during the forward pass, converting
noise-laden sequences into compact, information-rich representa-
tions. On the output side, Multi-step Auxiliary Prediction (MAP)
employs a multi-token objective to densify feedback, strengthen-
ing long-range dependency capture and ensuring robust learning
signals despite compressed inputs. Unifying input purification and
signal amplification, STAMP enhances both training efficiency and
representation capability. Experiments on public Amazon and large-
scale industrial datasets show STAMP achieves 1.23–1.38× speedup
and 17.2%–54.7% VRAM reduction while maintaining or improving
performance across multiple architectures.

Introduction
Generative Recommendation (GR) has emerged as a paradigm shift
in the field of recommender systems, reformulating the task as an
end-to-end sequence generation problem [17, 26, 34, 44]. However,
early frameworks relying on atomic item identifiers were limited by
the independence of unique IDs, which hinders information sharing.
This limitation often leads to vocabulary explosion and cold-start
problem for new items. To mitigate these issues, Semantic Identifier
(SID)-based Generative Recommendation (SID-GR) [9, 30, 35, 58]
decomposes each item ID into a sequence of SIDs. The shared
SID vocabulary across different items enhances the generalization
ability of such methods (e.g., cross-domain recommendation), which
is why it has gained attention recently.
Although SID-GR demonstrates a higher potential compared
to item ID-based GR, it also exposes two critical limitations that
hinder its practical deployment: (1) High resource consumption and
slow training speed [9]. Expanding an item ID into multiple SIDs
significantly increases the sequence length, leading to quadratic
growth in memory (VRAM) consumption and training time. (2)
Large performance fluctuations and low stability [16, 21, 31, 57].
The relationship between the granularity of SIDs and recommenda-
tion accuracy is non-monotonic and exhibits inconsistent patterns
across different models and datasets. Currently, research on SID-GR
remains in its early stages. Efforts have prioritized improving SID
informativeness [9, 41, 43, 46, 47, 50] and generation speed [10, 58],
while optimizations of the "GR" backbone aimed at mitigating these
specific inefficiencies remain relatively under-explored.
Intriguingly, our empirical analysis reveals that these two seem-
ingly disparate issues—training inefficiency and performance insta-
bility—are actually two symptoms of the same underlying pathol-
ogy: the Semantic Dilution Effect. We find that user intent is typ-
ically driven by a sparse subset of critical tokens within SIDs,

Viewed through the lens of Semantic Dilution, existing optimiza-
tion strategies from adjacent domains reveal inherent limitations. (1)
KV caching and Efficient attention kernels [7, 13, 23, 32, 55] mitigate
the computational burden of long-sequence decoding; however,
these are primarily optimized for the long-horizon inference of

by the massive retraining overhead required to capture volatile
user interests. (2) Token pruning [1, 3, 6, 18, 36, 52, 54] aims to skip
redundant inputs, but unlike NLP where non-informative particles
(e.g., stop words) are distinct, every token in a high-granularity SID
is a semantically dense identifier with complex inter-dependencies,
making it difficult to distinguish "salient" tokens from noise without
compromising representation quality [51]. Consequently, effective
pruning mechanisms tailored for SID-GR remain unexplored.
Guided by this unified root cause, we propose STAMP (Semantic
Trimming and Auxiliary Multi-step Prediction), a framework de-
signed to counteract Semantic Dilution Effect through a dual-end
optimization strategy. We argue that effective SID learning requires
simultaneously tackling low information density at the input and
sparse supervision signals at the output. On the input side, we in-
troduce Semantic Adaptive Pruning (SAP) to address Information
Non-uniformity. SAP functions as a dynamic filter, identifying to-
ken utility via semantic features and attention centrality to remove
redundancy during the forward pass [13, 55]. This directly miti-
gates computational overhead by converting noise-laden sequences
into compact, information-rich representations. On the output side,
we incorporate Multi-step Auxiliary Prediction (MAP) to address
Supervision Sparsity. Beyond standard next-token prediction, MAP
employs a multi-token objective [11] that densifies the feedback
loop. This mechanism independently strengthens the model’s abil-
ity to capture long-range dependencies, ensuring robust learning
signals even with compressed inputs [11, 29]. By unifying input
purification and signal amplification, STAMP achieves both high
training efficiency and enhanced representation capability.

We implemented STAMP on two representative architectures:
Encoder-Decoder (T5) and Decoder-Only (Qwen). Extensive ex-
periments on Amazon datasets and a large-scale industrial dataset
(AL-GR-Tiny) demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach. Re-
sults show that STAMP achieves a 1.23–1.38 speedup and reduces
VRAM usage by 17.2%–54.7% while maintaining recommendation
performance.
The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows,
• We identify the Semantic Dilution Effect as the shared root
cause of inefficiency and instability in SID-GR, representing
an early effort focused on dynamically restructuring the SID
learning process.
• We propose STAMP, a dual-end framework that couples in-
put purification with signal amplification to reconcile training
efficiency with model robustness.
• Comprehensive experiments across multiple architectures and
large-scale datasets validate STAMP as a viable solution for
industrial generative recommendation.

Preliminaries
2.1
SID-based Generative Recommendation
Generative Recommendation. Distinct from traditional discrim-
inative recommendation, which treats retrieval as a "ranking" task
by predicting interaction probabilities for candidate items, GR re-
formulates the recommendation task as an end-to-end sequence
generation problem. Formally, let U and I denote the set of users
and items. Given a user 𝑢∈U with a chronological interaction
history 𝐻𝑢= [𝑖1,𝑖2, . . . ,𝑖𝑡], the goal of GR is to learn a probabilistic
model 𝑃𝜃that directly generates the unique identifier of the tar-
get item 𝑖𝑡+1 in an autoregressive manner. This paradigm aligns
recommendation with standard language modeling, leveraging the
powerful reasoning and contextual understanding capabilities of
Large Language Models (LLMs) to model complex user preferences.
Semantic ID Tokenization. A fundamental challenge in GR is ef-
fectively mapping discrete items into a token space compatible with
language models. Traditional approaches relying on independent
atomic IDs often suffer from vocabulary explosion and the cold-
start problem. To overcome these limitations, SIDs have emerged

2.2
Finding 1: Information Non-uniformity
Existing research has consistently demonstrated that input data
contains significant redundancy in non-critical information [1, 6, 49,
51, 52, 54]. Eliminating such redundancy through techniques like
token pruning or merging not only substantially reduces computa-
tional overhead but also enhances model performance by improving
the input signal-to-noise ratio.
This phenomenon exhibits a unique structural amplification
in SID-GR. Unlike traditional recommendation that map items to
single atomic IDs, the SID mechanism utilizes hierarchical encoding
to decompose a single item into a sequence of multiple tokens.
While this design enhances semantic expressiveness, it expands the
sequence length from a single token to 𝐿tokens, thereby incurring
substantial ineffective computation. Specifically, SID introduces a
distinctively low density of effective information, characterized by
two primary types of redundancy:
(1) Feature-level Redundancy: User interaction history is of-
ten driven by a subset of key semantic features. For instance,
within the hierarchy Category →Brand →Attribute, a user
purchasing sneakers may exclusively prioritize the Brand to-
ken. Consequently, fine-grained Attribute information such
as color or material becomes irrelevant noise relative to the
current prediction task.
(2) Sequence-level Redundancy: Semantic overlaps exist
among SIDs within the same sequence (e.g., different items
may share the identical SIDs, such as the same brand), re-
sulting in diminishing marginal returns for information ex-
pression.
However, this fine-grained decomposition also offers new op-
portunities. SID explicitly decouples the mixed features within an
item into independent tokens. This separation provides a natural
structural foundation that allows the model to more easily locate
and strip away redundant features, facilitating efficient pruning.
2.3
Finding 2: Supervision Sparsity
In contrast to general generative tasks, where every generated
token serves as an immediate supervision signal, GR faces a unique
challenge regarding supervision sparsity: valid training signals
are derived solely from the ground-truth item at the end of the
sequence.
Although the SID mechanism extends the prediction target to 𝐿
SIDs, the input sequence expands synchronously by a factor of 𝐿.
Consequently, the relative density of supervision signals does not
improve. This sparsity exacerbates the difficulty of learning under
long-sequence conditions, making it challenging for the model to
precisely attribute the final purchasing behavior to specific key
features in the interaction history.
Furthermore, the SID mechanism introduces joint probability
constraints. In SID-GR, correctly predicting an item is equivalent to
accurately predicting its 𝐿corresponding SID tokens consecutively:

additional supervision signals is essential to enhance the model’s
capacity for long-range dependency modeling and induction.

We design the SAP by capitalizing on the Transformer’s intrinsic
self-attention mechanism. Since attention weights naturally quan-
tify the information flow between tokens, they serve as an effective,
computationally efficient proxy for token utility [13, 55]. This en-
ables the SAP to dynamically identify and retain discriminative
SIDs without incurring significant computational overhead.

4
Experiments
To comprehensively evaluate the versatility and robustness of
STAMP across different architectures and data scales, we con-
duct extensive experiments using a dual-track evaluation setting.
Specifically, we utilize the GRID [16] framework to implement
the Encoder-Decoder (T5) architecture on three public Amazon
datasets. Complementarily, to assess the framework’s capability in
a large-scale industrial scenario, we employ the FORGE [9] frame-
work to instantiate the Decoder-Only (Qwen) architecture on the
industrial dataset AL-GR-Tiny. Through this diverse setup, we aim
to answer the following research questions:
• RQ1: How does STAMP perform in performance and efficiency
on different architectures of SID-based generative recommen-
dation?
• RQ2: What roles do STAMP’s two modules, SAP and MAP,
play in its performance?
• RQ3: How do the design configurations of SAP (i.e., pruning
strategy, pruning layer, and retention ratio) affect the model
performance?
• RQ4: What mechanism enables STAMP to safely prune the
majority of tokens?

and Toys datasets. This finding suggests that STAMP acts as

core user preferences. Moreover, the richer supervision signals

effectiveness.

demonstrates that STAMP is model-agnostic, capable of effec-

the generative quality.

4.3.4
Why is Pruning Safe? (RQ4). To uncover the reason why we
can compress tokens significantly without performance loss, we
visualize the attention heatmaps of the T5 encoder (Layers 1 to 4)
for three randomly sampled sequences from the Beauty test set, as
shown in Figure 10. Consistent with the information aggregation
phenomenon observed in LLMs [12, 49], the attention patterns re-
veal a clear hierarchical information flow characterized by two key
observations. 1) a large portion of item tokens show consistently
low attention scores throughout the entire encoding process. These
tokens likely represent noisy interactions or redundant information
overlapped by other dominant tokens. Since they rarely participate
in the information exchange required for user profiling, they act
as "dead weight" and can be safely pruned to save computational
resources. 2) A distinct subset of tokens shows a dynamic pattern
where they receive high attention weights in early layers but fade
rapidly in deeper layers. This suggests that early layers use these to-
kens as essential scaffolding to build basic features, which are later
absorbed by specific key tokens. Crucially, this explains why prun-
ing should not be performed too early in shallow layers: removing
these tokens too early disrupts the initial information aggregation,
whereas pruning them later, once their semantic value has been
fully digested, is both safe and efficient.

Related Work
Generative Retrieval. Generative recommendation has recently
emerged as a transformative paradigm [17, 26, 34, 44]. Prior to
this paradigm shift, researchers primarily utilized LLMs as aux-
iliary components to enhance conventional discriminative mod-
els [4, 37, 40]. In contrast, the generative retrieval framework serial-
izes item interaction sequences into discrete tokens. This approach
allows the model to directly predict target items in an autoregres-
sive manner [9, 16, 51]. Building on this foundation, subsequent
studies have focused on refining the learning process by develop-
ing optimized training frameworks [22, 30] to handle large-scale
data. Furthermore, advanced strategies such as reinforcement learn-
ing [42, 56] have been integrated to better align generated outputs
with complex user preferences.
Semantic Identifier. The efficacy of generative recommendation
relies heavily on the tokenization strategy. The Semantic ID (SID)
paradigm, pioneered by TIGER [35], represents items as sequences
of hierarchical codewords. This structure enables knowledge trans-
fer among semantically similar entities. Early SID methods primar-
ily derived codes from content modalities. However, subsequent
research argued that content alone is insufficient for capturing user
preferences. Consequently, a growing body of work [20, 30, 45]
has shifted towards incorporating collaborative signals. Regarding
encoding efficiency [25], approaches like OneRec [58] and RPG [15]
have streamlined the quantization pipeline using Residual Quan-
tized K-means (RQ-Kmeans) and Optimized Product Quantization
(OPQ) [10] to effectively reduce the codebook size. More recently,
GRID [16] introduced a unified and open-source framework specif-
ically designed for SID-GR. FORGE [9] addressed the challenges of
industrial deployment by introducing a large-scale benchmark and
proposing advanced optimization strategies to ensure SID robust-
ness in real-world scenarios.
Acceleration of LLMRec. Extensive efforts have been made to ad-
dress the efficiency challenges of generative recommendation from
different perspectives. Regarding model complexity, techniques
like quantization [8, 24] and knowledge distillation [5, 39] effec-
tively reduce the memory usage. However, these approaches usually
impose additional post-training phases and often incur a loss in
model performance. To target latency reduction, researchers have
explored speculative sampling [27, 48] and efficient attention mech-
anisms [7, 23, 32, 53]. More recently, token pruning has been inves-
tigated as a promising direction to skip redundant computation in
NLP and CV [1, 6, 51, 52, 54]. Despite their potential, speculative
sampling and existing token pruning methods remain primarily
inference-centric.

Conclusion
In this work, we identified the Semantic Dilution Effect as the shared
root cause of inefficiency and instability in SID-based Generative
Recommendation, characterized by Information Non-uniformity at

Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY
Trovato et al.
the input and Supervision Sparsity at the output. To address this,
we proposed STAMP, a dual-end framework designed to reconcile
training efficiency with model robustness. Central to our approach
is the Semantic Adaptive Pruning (SAP) module, which dynami-
cally filters redundant tokens to substantially reduce computational
overhead and memory consumption. Furthermore, we integrated
a Multi-step Auxiliary Prediction (MAP) module. Rather than sim-
ply acting as a remedy for pruning, MAP serves as an indepen-
dent enhancement that densifies supervision signals. This enables
the model to capture long-range dependencies and learn robust
user representations. Extensive experiments across diverse datasets
and backbone architectures (Encoder-Decoder and Decoder-Only)
demonstrate that STAMP achieves an optimal balance between
efficiency and performance. The results confirm that STAMP de-
livers significant training acceleration and memory savings while
maintaining, and in some cases surpassing, the recommendation
accuracy of full-sequence baselines.
