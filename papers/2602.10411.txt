Abstract
Next Point-of-Interest (POI) prediction is a fundamental task in
location-based services, especially critical for large-scale naviga-
tion platforms like AMAP that serve billions of users across diverse
lifestyle scenarios. While recent POI recommendation approaches
based on SIDs have achieved promising, they struggle in complex,
sparse real-world environments due to two key limitations: (1) in-
adequate modeling of high-quality SIDs that capture cross-category
spatio-temporal collaborative relationships, and (2) poor alignment
between large language models (LLMs) and the POI recommenda-
tion task. To this end, we propose GeoGR, a geographic generative
recommendation framework tailored for navigation-based LBS like
AMAP, which perceives users’ contextual state changes and enables
intent-aware POI recommendation. GeoGR features a two-stage de-
sign: (i) a geo-aware SID tokenization pipeline that explicitly learns
spatio-temporal collaborative semantic representations via geo-
graphically constrained co-visited POI pairs, contrastive learning,
and iterative refinement; and (ii) a multi-stage LLM training strategy
that aligns non-native SID tokens through multiple template-based
continued pre-training(CPT) and enables autoregressive POI gener-
ation via supervised fine-tuning(SFT). Extensive experiments on
multiple real-world datasets demonstrate GeoGR’s superiority over
state-of-the-art baselines. Moreover, deployment on the AMAP
platform, serving millions of users with multiple online metrics
boosting, confirms its practical effectiveness and scalability in pro-
duction.

Introduction
Next Point-of-Interest (POI) prediction [1, 5, 15, 27, 29, 37] aims
to forecast the future POIs a user is likely to visit based on their
historical trajectories and contextual signals (e.g., time, location,
and activity patterns). This task is especially critical for navigation-
centric, large-scale LBS platforms such as AMAP, which integrates
a wide range of services—including navigation, dining, accommo-
dation, tourism, and fueling—and serves billions of users. In such
complex ecosystems, accurate POI prediction is essential not only
for efficient route planning but also for delivering personalized,
context-aware recommendations under highly sparse, dynamic,
and heterogeneous usage conditions [37].
Human mobility exhibits strong spatiotemporal continuity and
regularity, with points of interest (POIs) often forming meaningful
correlations that extend beyond simple sequential co-occurrence.
Traditional approaches frame next-POI prediction as a spatiotempo-
ral sequential recommendation task, employing specialized architec-
tures to capture large-scale user behavior patterns [10, 11, 23, 25, 33].
Recently, large language models (LLMs) have been increasingly
adopted for their powerful reasoning and generalization capabil-
ities, enabling more nuanced modeling of user intent by fusing
historical trajectories with personal preferences and contextual
cues [2, 7, 34, 38, 41]. However, most LLM-based methods still repre-
sent POIs using either randomly assigned non-semantic identifiers
or text-derived embeddings that inadequately capture collabora-
tive relationships—particularly cross-category associations (e.g.,
between airports, hotels, and parking lots) that emerge from real-
world behavioral semantics and shared user intents. Generative
Recommendation (GR) [9, 21, 36] has emerged as a promising para-
digm by rethinking POI tokenization: rather than assigning each
POI a unique atomic identifier, GR encodes it into a short sequence

a shared, compact vocabulary. Target POIs are then generated au-

language models (LLMs). While this design significantly improves
memory efficiency and system scalability, the effectiveness of GR
hinges critically on the quality of the SIDs. In particular, their abil-
ity to capture both semantic meaning and collaborative user–POI
interaction patterns during encoding remains a key challenge that
is largely unaddressed in existing frameworks [8, 16].
Although SID-based GR has achieved promising results, exist-
ing approaches still struggle to effectively encode SIDs and per-
form POI recommendation in complex, sparse scenarios—such
as navigation-centric omni-scenario LBS platforms (e.g., AMAP),
which integrate diverse interactions including navigation, dining,
accommodation, tourism, and so on. Such environments demand
a more sophisticated understanding of user intent under varying
spatio-temporal contexts, e.g., “Given a user’s(who) search(what) at
a specific time(When) and location(where), what is their underlying
goal, destination, and likely next action(how)?” We identify two key
challenges in this setting: (1) Inadequate modeling of high-quality
SIDs. Current methods typically treat the LLM as an encoder that de-
rives semantic representations solely from POI textual descriptions
to construct SIDs. While the LLM’s strong semantic comprehension
ensures similar POIs receive similar embeddings, this paradigm
overlooks collaborative relationships among POIs, especially cross-
category associations (e.g., between airport, hotel and parking lot).
In LBS and recommendation systems, strong associations often ex-
ist between POIs of different categories, typically arising from real
user behavior patterns, geographic proximity, functional comple-
mentarity, or coupling within everyday life scenarios. Even when
auxiliary signals like co-visit IDs or geographic distances are in-
jected into the input, the LLM often fails to effectively capture
these structured dependencies—and may even suffer from diluted
semantic representations due to information interference. Hence,
it is crucial to explicitly incorporate spatiotemporal collaborative
signals during semantic modeling, rather than relying on the LLM’s
passive learning of implicit patterns. (2) Insufficient alignment be-
tween the base LLM and the recommendation task. The base LLM
is neither pre-trained on recommendation-specific knowledge nor
inherently compatible with the newly introduced SID tokens. Di-
rectly fine-tuning the model on a single instruction-tuning dataset is
therefore suboptimal, as it fails to fully adapt the LLM’s generative
capabilities to the POI recommendation domain.
To address the aforementioned challenges, we propose GeoGR,
a condition-aware GR framework tailored for navigation-based
LBS scinarios. Training GeoGR comprises two core stages: (1) POI
SID construction: Learning high-quality SID tokens by integrating
spatial-aware semantic representations, textual attributes, and ge-
ographically constrained co-visited POI pairs. (2) Generative POI
recommendation: fine-tuning a LLM via a multi-stage alignment
procedure—comprising CPTand SFT—to autoregressively generate
POI SIDs conditioned on user context. In the SID construction stage,
we design a three-step approach to fully exploit semantic and spatio-
temporal collaborative associations: First, we learn spatio-temporal
collaborativesSemantic representation by constructing geograph-
ically constrained co-visited POI pairs and applying contrastive
learning enhanced with LLM-based semantic encoding. Second,
we tokenize these representations by generating dense semantic
embeddings via an LLM and applying RQ-Kmeans [4, 16] to derive
discrete, hierarchical SIDs. Third, we refine the SIDs using an EM-
style iterative optimization algorithm. In the generative training
stage, we adapt the LLM to the next-POI prediction task through
a CPT–SFT pipeline. Since SIDs are non-native tokens to the base
LLM, we first perform CPT using a mixture of template-based Text-
to-SID prompt datasets to align the LLM’s vocabulary with the POI
recommendation domain. We evaluate GeoGR on multiple public
benchmarks and deploy it on the AMAP platform, where it serves
millions of daily active users. Online A/B tests demonstrate signifi-
cant improvements across key engagement metrics, confirming its
effectiveness and scalability in real-world industrial settings.
The major contributions are summarized as follows:
• We propose GeoGR, a novel geographic GR framework for navigation-
based LBS, which unifies semantic ID learning and autoregressive
POI generation within a LLM paradigm.
• We design a geo-aware SID tokenization pipeline that aims to
enhance the POI SID with spatio-temporal collaborative infor-
mation, geographically constrained co-visited pairs, contrastive
learning, and an EM-style iterative refinement algorithm.
• We introduce a multi-stage alignment and fine-tuning strategy
to adapt LLMs to the next POI recommendation task, including
CPT-based template alignment for non-native SID tokens and
SFT for autoregressive POI generation.
• Extensive experiments on offline and online experiments demon-
strate the remarkable effectiveness of GeoGR.

Related Works
POI Recommendation. Traditional next-POI recommendation
methods are typically formulated as a sequential recommenda-
tion task, where the goal is to predict a user’s next visited loca-
tion based on their historical check-in sequence [11, 13, 24, 28, 35].
Early approaches leveraged Markov chains or matrix factorization
to model transition patterns or user–POI affinities but struggled
to capture long-term dependencies and complex spatio-temporal
dynamics. With the advent of deep learning, recurrent neural net-
works (RNNs)—particularly LSTM and GRU variants—were widely
adopted to model long-term user preferences and sequential de-
pendencies [6]. Subsequently, transformer-based models, powered
by self-attention mechanisms, demonstrated superior performance
over RNNs in next-POI recommendation [11]. For instance, STAN [18]
employs self-attention to explicitly model pairwise spatio-temporal

relationships among POIs, capturing both non-consecutive tran-
sitions and geographic proximity through interpolated distance
encoding. More recently, the integration of large language models
(LLMs) has opened new frontiers in semantic-aware location mod-
eling. LLM4POI [13] fine-tunes LLMs on standard-scale datasets to
leverage commonsense knowledge for next-POI prediction.
Generative Recommendation. Beyond discriminative mod-
els, generative approaches [14, 30, 37] have emerged as a pow-
erful paradigm for recommendation by directly generating item
identifiers—thereby eliminating the need for multi-stage cascaded
pipelines. TIGER [22] pioneers a two-stage generative framework:
it first learns hierarchical SIDs, then generates candidate items
via autoregressive decoding. Common quantization methods for
learning SIDs include RQ-VAE [12], RQ-Kmeans [4, 22]. By lever-
aging hierarchical codewords, SIDs effectively mitigate vocabulary
sparsity and long-tail challenges in large-scale recommendation
systems. In industrial practice, KuaiShou proposes OneRec [4] and
OneRec-V2 [39], which employ reinforcement learning (RL) [30]
for user preference modeling and unify cascaded retrieval–ranking
pipelines into an end-to-end generative framework. GNPR-SID [28]
introduces a generative next-POI recommendation model that inte-
grates SIDs to jointly capture intrinsic POI semantics, collaborative
signals, and user visit patterns. In the local lifestyle services domain,
KuaiShou further develops OneLoc [30], an end-to-end generative
recommender that fully leverages geographic information.

Methods
We first present an overview of the GeoGR training pipeline (as
illustrated in Figure 2), followed by a detailed description of its two
core stages: constructing geo-aware POI semantic IDs and training
the generative POI recommendation model.
4.1
Framework Overview
Similar to existing GR models, GeoGR follows a two-stage pipeline.
Its core innovation lies in encoding the rich semantics of POIs
into compact, discrete tokens that seamlessly integrate with LLMs,
thereby enhancing their ability to understand user intent based on
contextual conditions, search queries, and mobility trajectories.
In the first stage, we encode textual and geographic information
for each POI—including longitude, latitude, location address, and
contextual descriptors (e.g., name, brand, category)—into a set of
specialized location SIDs using hierarchical vector quantization
techniques such as RQ-VAE and RQ-Kmeans. The resulting SIDs
preserve rich semantic information while ensuring that semanti-
cally similar POIs are mapped to nearby representations through
the hierarchical codebook structure. More importantly, a compact
set of codebook tokens can efficiently represent a vast number of
POIs, circumventing the vocabulary explosion problem that arises
when introducing excessive new tokens into LLMs. Furthermore,
the short length and well-defined structure of SIDs improve fine-
tuning stability, leading to more accurate POI generation.
In the second stage, we train the LLM to progressively under-
stand the recommendation task and ultimately perform condition-
aware retrieval and prediction of the next POI. First, we conduct
CPT to align the newly introduced SID tokens with POI and recom-
mendation corpus based on multiple pre-defined template-based
corpus data. Building upon this CPT-LLM, we construct an instruction-
tuning dataset specifically designed for next-location prediction
and further refine the model via supervised fine-tuning. Through
this process, GeoGR achieves a deep understanding of user behavior
and delivers high-quality next-POI recommendations.
4.2
Geo-Aware POI Semantic ID
Most existing LLM-based POI recommendation methods rely on
randomly assigned identifiers (RIDs), which lack semantic meaning
and fail to capture user intent in contextual scenarios. Moreover,
such random encoding drastically increases model storage require-
ments, hindering scalability and inference efficiency. To address
these limitations, we adopt an RQ-kmeans based semantic tokeniza-
tion strategy [4] that leverages POI textual descriptions to learn a
coarse-to-fine hierarchical codebook through autoregressive mod-
eling. By using a reduced, fixed-size set of tokens, our approach
alleviates storage and computational overhead while enabling seam-
less integration of new POIs—each mapped to semantically similar
existing tokens—thereby improving generalization. Given the strin-
gent requirement for geographic precision in navigation scenarios,
our tokenization strategy comprises three key steps: (1) learning
spatio-temporal collaborative semantic aware POI representations,
(2) semantic representation okenization, and (3) SID optimization
via an EM-style[3] algorithm. These steps collectively enhance the

Spatio-temporal Collaborative Semantic Representation. Here,
we aim to map each POI description into a POI representation vec-
tor for subsequent SID generation. To fully encode both semantic
and geographic information, we construct an informative location

attributes such as name, brand, categories, geohash code, consump-
tion level, active time period, among others. Existing approaches
typically feed POI textual descriptions into a pre-trained LLM to ob-
tain semantic embeddings. While semantically similar POIs tend to
yield close representations—and local context can help capture spa-
tial proximity—such purely semantic encoding suffers from two key
limitations: (1) it overlooks POI pairs that exhibit strong spatiotem-

the encoding of the target POI’s intrinsic semantics by attending

4.3
Generative POI Recommendation
In the previous steps, we illustrate the process of generating POI
SIDs. Building on those SIDs, we aim to propose a generative POI
recommendation framework based on the LLMs, such as Qwen
4B[31]. To achieve this, we adopt an instruction-tuning paradigm,
which finetunes LLM with high-quality, domain-specific datasets.
Since those added SID tokens are essentially out-of-vocabulary
tokens that LLMs do not recognize, we first apply the continued
pre-training(CPT) optimizaion for aligning the recommendation
corpus with the added SID tokens. After continued pre-training, the
model typically acquires an understanding of the domain-specific
knowledge base as well as the mobility patterns underlying POIs
and user behavior. Then, based on the CPT-LLM, we fine-tune the
LLM using cross-scenario navigation behavior. Here, we specifi-
cally focus on next POI generation task, ensuring that the training
instructions are consistent with those used during online inference.

5.6
Online Experiments
GeoGR has been deployed on the AMAP platform to power user
query retrieval on the homepage, replacing the conventional multi-
stage recall and coarse-ranking pipeline. When a user submits a
query, the prompt construction module retrieves user-specific data
from the database—including personalized preferences and histori-
cal behavior trajectories—and combines it with real-time contextual
signals (e.g., current location, timestamp, and search query) to form
a complete input prompt. GeoGR then generates 50 candidate SID
using dynamic beam search. After necessary post-processing, these
candidates are passed to downstream fine-ranking and re-ranking
modules to produce the final recommendations. To balance efficacy
and inference efficiency in production, we identified GeoGR-0.6B
as the optimal model variant. It delivers competitive recommenda-
tion quality while maintaining a manageable latency: an average
of 50 ms per request on NVIDIA H20 GPU, compared to 90 ms
for GeoGR-4B. Consequently, we deployed GeoGR-0.6B in produc-
tion and conducted a one-week online A/B test against the legacy
multi-stage retrieval baseline.
As shown in Table 6, we report online performance across multi-
ple scenarios, including food, lifestyle services, and tourism. GeoGR
achieves consistent and significant improvements across key busi-
ness metrics: WinRate +2.32%, P_CTR +1.50%, PV_CTR +2.25%,
UV_CTR +0.98%. WinRate is a composite metric that quantifies
overall user engagement gains by weighting strategic actions—such

free location-based services at scale.

Conclusion
In this work, we present GeoGR, a novel generative recommenda-
tion framework tailored for large-scale POI retrieval in navigation-
based LBS scenarios. To generate geo-aware semantic POI SID, we
start with fine-tuning the embedding encoder model based on the
sampled geo-constrained co-visited POI pairs, and then apply the
RQ-Kmeans and EM-style algorithms for generating and refining
the SIDs. For training a condition-aware generative recommen-
dation system in industrial application, we introduce a CPT and
SFT fine-tuning procedure. The process first aligns the added SID
tokens with multiple template prompts and then enhancing the
optimal effectiveness of SFT with next POI prediction dataset. Ge-
oGR has been successfully deployed in the AMAP platform, and
achieved significant improvement in multiple metrics. In the future,
we plan to further enhance GeoGR’s reasoning and recommenda-
tion capabilities through approaches such as scaling up the model
size, extending the length of user behavior sequences, constructing
Chain-of-Thought (CoT)-based training data, and incorporating
reinforcement learning (RL) for optimization.
