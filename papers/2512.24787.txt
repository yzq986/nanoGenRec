Abstract
Slate recommendation, which presents users with a ranked item
list in a single display, is ubiquitous across mainstream online plat-
forms. Recent advances in generative models have shown signifi-
cant potential for this task via autoregressive modeling of discrete
semantic ID sequences. However, existing methods suffer from
three key limitations: entangled item tokenization, inefficient se-
quential decoding, and the absence of holistic slate planning. These
issues often result in substantial inference overhead and inadequate
alignment with diverse user preferences and practical business re-
quirements, hindering the industrial deployment of generative slate
recommendation systems. In this paper, we propose HiGR, an effi-
cient generative slate recommendation framework that integrates
hierarchical planning with listwise preference alignment. First, we
design an auto-encoder incorporating residual quantization and
contrastive constraints, which tokenizes items into semantically
structured IDs to enable controllable generation. Second, HiGR de-
couples the generation process into two stages: a list-level planning
stage to capture global slate intent, and an item-level decoding stage
to select specific items, effectively reducing the search space and
enabling efficient generation. Third, we introduce a multi-objective
and listwise preference alignment mechanism that enhances slate
quality by leveraging implicit user feedback. Extensive experiments
have validated the effectiveness of our HiGR method. Notably, it

Introduction
Personalized recommendation systems have emerged as crucial
tools for information filtering and content distribution in large-scale
online services, underpinning user experiences across news feeds,
short-video platforms, and e-commerce applications [2, 20, 32, 38].
In contrast to conventional item-level recommendation [1] that
delivers items individually, slate recommendation presents users
with a ranked list of items simultaneously, with representative
applications including YouTube’s homepage feed, TikTok’s For You
Page, and Amazon’s product recommendation lists. As the primary
carrier for information delivery, a slate goes beyond individual item
selection. Its composition and arrangement directly influence the

efficiency of information discovery, user satisfaction, and long-term
engagement with the platform.
Existing slate recommendation approaches typically adhere to a
two-stage paradigm: first, scoring candidate items independently
via point-wise or pair-wise ranking models [18], then assembling
the final slate through greedy selection or reranking heuristics.
While computationally efficient, these methods have two critical
drawbacks: 1) They optimize item-level objectives rather than list-
level quality, and thus fail to capture the synergistic relationships
between items within a slate. For instance, in short-video recom-
mendation scenarios, users prefer diverse content that balances
entertainment, information, and novelty, which is a holistic prefer-
ence beyond the reach of independent item scoring. 2) The greedy
assembly process lacks look-ahead and backtracking capabilities.
Even with precise item-level scores, the resulting slate is often sub-
optimal, as early outputs constrain subsequent choices without a
global optimization perspective.
Recent advances in generative models [8], especially Large Lan-
guage Models (LLMs) and autoregressive architectures, offer new
insights for slate recommendation. By formulating a recommen-
dation as a sequence generation task, these approaches can model
complex dependencies between items and generate coherent rec-
ommendation lists end-to-end [9]. While theoretically viable, di-
rectly adapting existing methods to slate recommendation scenarios
struggles to ensure satisfactory generation quality and inference
efficiency. To achieve effective deployment on large-scale online
platforms, three key challenges need to be addressed:
• Semantically entangled item tokenization: To enable item gener-
ation, recent works leverage Semantic IDs (SID) from Residual
Quantized VAEs (RQ-VAE) [27] for item tokenization into dis-
crete codes. However, current semantic quantization methods
often yield an entangled SID space where “different prefixes carry
similar semantics” or “identical prefixes imply distinct meanings.”
This lack of clear semantic boundaries hinders precise generation
control and compromises the consistency of output quality.
• Inefficient slate generation: While semantic IDs reduce vocabulary
size, they require encoding each item as multiple tokens (e.g., 3
tokens per item). For slate recommendation tasks, this means
autoregressive models must generate these tokens sequentially,
with each step dependent on all prior outputs. A typical 10-item
slate, for instance, demands 30 sequential generation steps. This
serial process drastically slows inference speed, creating a critical
bottleneck for systems with sub-100ms latency requirements.
• Absence of holistic slate planning: Existing autoregressive meth-
ods lack explicit mechanisms for global list planning. Although
they can theoretically condition each item on the preceding con-
text, they still follow a left-to-right generation paradigm without
dedicated modules for optimizing the slate structure. This fre-
quently results in slates that are locally coherent but globally
suboptimal: later items may conflict with earlier selections, or
fail to satisfy key list-level objectives such as diversity, coverage,
and topical coherence.
To tackle these challenges, we propose HiGR (Hierarchical
Generative Slate Recommendation), a novel framework that for-
mulates slate recommendation as a hierarchical coarse-to-fine gen-
eration task with listwise preference alignment. Specifically, to
simultaneously ensure high-quality slate generation and efficient in-
ference, we design a comprehensive optimization solution spanning
three core dimensions: item semantic tokenization, slate generation
architecture, and model post-training.
First, to alleviate semantic entanglement, we design the Con-
trastive Residual Quantized Variational Auto-Encoder (CRQ-VAE).
It integrates prefix-level contrastive constraints into the hierarchi-
cal residual quantization pipeline, compelling high-level ID prefixes
to explicitly encode semantic similarity and separability. This re-
sults in a structured vocabulary where the “prefix” serves as a
reliable semantic anchor, laying a solid foundation for subsequent
hierarchical generation. Second, we propose the Hierarchical Slate
Decoder (HSD), which decouples slate generation into two syner-
gistic stages: a list-level preference planning stage for capturing
the global structure and intent of the entire slate, followed by an
item-level decoding stage for grounding these preference plans
into specific item selections. This coarse-to-fine decomposition
parallels human decision-making logic: first defining the overall
composition and themes, then populating the slate with specific
items. Such a hierarchical design effectively reduces search space,
ensuring efficient inference and coherent global planning. Finally,
we introduce a listwise and multi-objective preference alignment
mechanism to enforce holistic planning and bridge the gap between
training objectives and user experience. Unlike existing RL-based
approaches that rely on item-level rewards, we adopt Odds Ratio
Preference Optimization (ORPO) to directly optimize the entire
slate structure. By constructing multi-objective preference pairs
that contrast effective slates against negatives, we jointly optimize
for ranking fidelity, genuine interest, and diversity. This enables
the generated slates to be not only accurate, but also structurally
coherent and diverse. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
• We propose HiGR, the first end-to-end generative slate recom-
mendation framework that significantly boosts both inference
efficiency and recommendation performance, enabling its deploy-
ment on large-scale online platforms.
• We present CRQ-VAE to enforce prefix-level alignment through
contrastive learning, and propose HSD to decouple list-level plan-
ning from item-level selection. This dual design enables efficient
slate generation while maintaining coherent global planning.
• We introduce a listwise preference alignment mechanism that
leverages implicit user feedback to enhance slate quality in the
post-training phase, effectively aligning model training with mul-
tiple real-world evaluation metrics.
• Extensive experiments have validated the superiority of HiGR in
both recommendation quality and inference speed. Additionally,
we have deployed HiGR on the Tencent platform (serving hun-
dreds of millions of users), achieving a 1.22% increase in Average
Watch Time and a 1.73% increase in Average Video Plays, which
significantly enhances user satisfaction.

Related Work
2.1
Slate Recommendation
Standard pointwise recommendation methods [5, 6, 11, 14, 17, 24,
28] evaluate relevance scores for each user-item pair independently.
While effective in predicting individual interactions (e.g., CTR),
they neglect inter-item dependencies and the relative order within

a list. In contrast, slate recommendation treats the entire list as the
fundamental unit to capture global context. For instance, ListCVAE
[16] employs Conditional Variational Autoencoders to model the
joint distribution of recommendation lists, encoding both positional
biases and complex inter-item dependencies within a latent space.
GFN4Rec [19] adapts Generative Flow Networks to recommenda-
tion, modeling the list generation as a sequential flow where the
probability of sampling a list is proportional to its expected reward.
DMSG [29] leverages conditional diffusion models to synthesize co-
herent and diverse item slates guided by natural language prompts,
thereby achieving precise controllability and semantic alignment
in list-wise recommendation. However, these methods often rely
on reranking paradigms that induce cascading errors or suffer from
training instability due to complex optimization objectives. In con-
trast, our work reformulates slate construction as an end-to-end
autoregressive sequence generation task. This paradigm facilitates
efficient global planning and leverages the scaling properties of
LLMs for stable convergence and industrial applicability.
2.2
Generative Recommendation
Inspired by LLMs, recommendation systems are shifting from dis-
criminative to generative modeling [7, 15, 35, 36], which formulates
tasks as sequence-to-sequence problems. Recent advances focus on
efficiency and scalability: HSTU [34] replaces standard attention
mechanisms with gated linear recurrence to accelerate training and
inference; MTGR [12] builds an industrial-scale generative frame-
work on HSTU, effectively combining the scalability of generative
models with the precision of traditional DLRMs via cross-feature
preservation and group-layer normalization. Furthermore, One-
Trans [37] enhances multi-task learning and knowledge transfer,
enabling the generative model to generalize better across diverse
recommendation scenarios.
Generative recommendation typically incorporates items as to-
kens within the model context [4], but extensive items will create a
large vocabulary size and exacerbate cold-start issues. To address
this problem, Semantic IDs derived via vector quantization have
emerged to enable a fixed-size vocabulary while preserving seman-
tic information. For example, TIGER [27] utilizes residual-quantized
variational auto-encoders (RQ-VAE) to recursively encode items
by self-supervised learning and embedding reconstruction, facil-
itating effective knowledge transfer to cold-start items. LETTER
[31] introduces a learnable tokenization framework to optimize
codebooks end-to-end for sequential recommendation. OneRec
[9] employs iterative K-means to generate hierarchical IDs, offer-
ing superior computational efficiency. However, existing methods
mainly align features before/after quantization, leading to structural
misalignment. We propose a Contrastive RQ-VAE, which enforces
prefix-level feature alignment within the quantization process, en-
hancing the interpretability and controllability of our hierarchical
slate generation process.
2.3
Reinforcement Learning for Generative
Recommendation
Reinforcement Learning (RL) is often used to align next-token pre-
diction with business metrics. To avoid brittle hand-crafted rewards,
PrefRec [33] adopts a paradigm similar to Reinforcement Learning
from Human Feedback (RLHF) [23], training a reward model on
user historical preferences to guide the policy toward long-term
engagement. DPO4Rec [26] stabilizes training by adapting pairwise
preference learning to sequence models, converting the alignment
problem into a stable supervised loss that complements or replaces
complex online RL loops. Recent industrial advancements demon-
strate the scalability of these techniques. OneRec-V1 [9] unifies
retrieval and ranking with a generative backbone with iterative
DPO for preference alignment. Then, OneRec-V2 [39] incorporates
duration-sensitive reward shaping and adaptive ratio clipping to
optimize user engagement from real-time feedback, advancing fully
aligned generative recommendation. Despite their success, RLHF
and DPO rely on auxiliary reference models, imposing heavy mem-
ory and computational cost. Moreover, generic alignment objectives
often fail to capture the multi-dimensional requirements of slate
generation (e.g., diversity). To address this, we introduce Odds Ra-
tio Preference Optimization (ORPO). By eliminating the reference
model and enforcing a “triple-objective” (ranking, interest, and di-
versity) directly in the supervised loss, we achieve a highly efficient
and stable training process tailored for complex slate preferences.

3.3
Contrastive RQ-VAE (CRQ-VAE)
While semantic quantization methods (e.g., RQ-VAE and RQ-KMeans)
can produce SIDs in a coarse-to-fine manner, adapting them for
slate recommendation tasks in large-scale industrial settings have to
solve the following problems: 1) ID Entanglement and Sparsity:
Overcomplete codebooks often result in a highly sparse and entan-
gled ID space, causing polysemy or synonymy in ID prefixes and
degrading the interpretability and controllability of IDs; 2) Lack
of Collaborative Alignment: Optimization via reconstruction
and residual minimization alone ignores the relational structure
between items. Without contrastive constraints, similar samples
are not encouraged to share or cluster around common ID prefixes,
while dissimilar samples are not separated, weakening the gen-
erator’s capacity to capture collaborative signals; 3) Inefficient
Diversity Control: Diversity is evaluated only after generation
by comparing the continuous embeddings aggregated from multi-
ple codebook layers. This prevents imposing direct, per-position
constraints on SIDs during the quantization process.
To address these issues, we propose a Contrastive Residual Quan-
tized Variational Auto-Encoder (CRQ-VAE). As shown in Figure
1(a), CRQ-VAE integrates hierarchical quantization with contrastive
learning to ensure that the resulting IDs are semantically rich and
structurally collaborative. Detailed design is presented as follows.

3.4
Hierarchical Slate Decoder (HSD)
An intuitive generative slate recommendation paradigm treats each
item’s SID as a token, trains the model with the Next-Token Predic-
tion (NTP) loss, and autoregressively generates subsequent items
from previous ones during inference. However, as each item is rep-
resented by 𝐷SIDs, generating an 𝑀-item slate requires 𝐷× 𝑀
inference steps. This considerable computational overhead induces
excessive latency, rendering it infeasible for real-time recommen-
dation scenarios. Furthermore, token-by-token decoding lacks an
explicit hierarchical structure, entangling intra-item representa-
tions with inter-item transition patterns and thus failing to capture
complex and holistic list-level dependencies.
To ensure quality and efficiency in slate generation, we propose
a hierarchical slate decoder (HSD) with a coarse-to-fine paradigm.
As illustrated in Figure 2, HSD comprises a coarse-grained slate
planner (capturing the slate’s global structure and intent) and a
fine-grained item generator (instantiating preference plans into
concrete item selections). Given user features 𝑢and historical be-
haviors 𝑆𝑢, we first derive the context embedding 𝐶= E(𝑢,𝑆𝑢)
from the context encoder (in Figure 1(b)). This embedding 𝐶en-
capsulates historical interactions and serves as keys and values
for cross-attention modeling in both the slate planner and item
generator, enabling the generation of the final slate.
3.4.1

ence embeddings of preceding items in the target slate as input, and
autoregressively generates the preference embedding for the next

3.4.3
Greedy-Slate Beam-Item Inference. To balance inference ef-
fectiveness and efficiency, we propose a Greedy-Slate Beam-Item
(GSBI) strategy. Specifically, during inference, conditioned on the
predicted preference embedding from the coarse-grained slate plan-
ner, the fine-grained item generator performs beam search decoding
(with beam width 𝐵) to autoregressively generate the SID sequence
for each item. The decoding of the 𝑀items is performed indepen-
dently: each item generator decodes its SID sequence exclusively
based on its corresponding preference embedding, eliminating de-
pendencies on the intermediate decoding states of other items in the
slate. Furthermore, the preference embedding of each item within a
slate is also generated autoregressively by the slate planner. At each
time step, the planner’s input is constructed by aggregating the
embeddings of the optimal SID sequence, which is greedily selected
as the top-1 candidate from the beam search results of the preceding
item generator. This GSBI inference strategy substantially boosts
slate generation efficiency, enabling its practical deployment in
large-scale industrial recommendation scenarios.
3.5
Multi-objective Preference Alignment
Recently, RL-based post-training has been widely adopted to en-
hance model performance. However, existing approaches overem-
phasize item-level preference alignment, leaving them susceptible
to historical exposure bias and unable to capture the holistic and
comparative nature of user slate evaluation. To enhance slate gen-
eration quality, we propose a listwise preference alignment mecha-
nism that jointly optimizes three core objectives: ranking fidelity,
genuine user interest, and slate diversity.
Specifically, we adopt Odds Ratio Preference Optimization (ORPO)
[13] as our reinforcement learning strategy due to two key advan-
tages: (1) As a reference-model-free method, it halves forward-pass
computation, significantly reduces GPU memory usage and enables
high-throughput streaming training; (2) By integrating preference
alignment with supervised learning, it mitigates objective drift and
anchors the model to its pretrained semantic distribution. Further-
more, we align model preferences with listwise user feedback: we
construct preference pairs from collected implicit slate-level feed-
back (e.g., watch time, completion rate, user satisfaction) and align
HiGR’s learning objective directly with key online business metrics.
3.5.1
Construction of Slate-Level Reference pairs. We construct
slate-level reference pairs (𝑦+,𝑦−) to support “triple-objective” op-
timization. Positive slates (𝑦+): Rerank a user’s engaged watch
sequence in descending order based on true user feedback. Nega-
tive slates (𝑦−) are designed according to our three optimization
objectives, respectively:
• Randomly permute 𝑦+ to disrupt its optimal ordering;
• Replace items in𝑦with those receiving negative feedback to filter
exposure noise;
• Anchor the first item in 𝑦+ and append top-(𝑀−1) semantically
dissimilar items to discourage repetitive recommendations and
“information cocoons”, thereby promoting slate diversity.
By performing contrastive optimization between 𝑦+ and these di-
verse negatives, our HiGR model learns to reject suboptimal slates
that are poorly ranked, irrelevant, or repetitive.

Online A/B Tests (RQ5)
We conducted online A/B tests on Tencent’s commercial media plat-
form, which serves tens of millions of Daily Active Users (DAUs).
Specifically, we allocated 2% of live traffic to benchmark HiGR
against the incumbent multi-stage recommender system. Our eval-
uation employs four key metrics: Average Stay Time and Average
Watch Time per user to quantify temporal engagement, alongside
Average Video Views and Average Request Count per user
to capture interaction frequency and content consumption. The
online results show that HiGR achieves 1.03% and 1.22% relative
improvements in Average Stay Time and Average Watch Time,
respectively, along with 1.73% and 1.57% relative improvements
in Average Video Views and Average Request Count. These gains
confirm that HiGR delivers superior recommendation performance,
which translates into significant improvements in the platform’s
core business metrics.
5
Conclusion
We present HiGR, a hierarchical generative framework for efficient
and high-quality slate recommendation. By leveraging CRQ-VAE
for structured semantic tokenization and employing a coarse-to-fine
hierarchical decoding strategy, our model successfully addresses the
critical challenges of low inference efficiency and inadequate global
planning in prior autoregressive approaches. Furthermore, incorpo-
rating listwise preference alignment ensures to generate slates that
maximize overall user satisfaction. Extensive experiments across
public dataset and an industrial dataset of our large-scale com-
mercial platform, involving both offline evaluations and online
A/B tests, confirm that HiGR consistently outperforms state-of-the-
art methods, delivering significant gains in both effectiveness and
efficiency. We believe that this work sets a solid foundation for next-
generation generative recommendation, successfully reconciling
high-fidelity generation with low-latency inference.

A
Implementation Details
• Model Architecture. We set the hidden dimension of cross/self
attention layers to 𝑑model = 512, and the feed-forward network
dimension to 𝑑FFN = 2048. Our proposed CRQ-VAE module uses
𝐷= 3 quantization layers and a codebook size of 1024. The archi-
tecture incorporates 𝑙slate = 14 slate planners and 𝑙item = 2 item
generators. These layer counts are set as the default configuration,
unless specified otherwise in ablation studies.
• Hyperparameters and Configuration. We optimize the model
using the Adam optimizer with a batch size of 4096. Both the
learning rate and weight decay are set to 1×10−4. During the pos-
itive samples construction process of our CRQ-VAE module, we
apply a cosine similarity threshold of 0.8 to distinguish positive
sample 𝑥𝑝from the negative sample 𝑥𝑛. The loss function coeffi-
cients for CRQ-VAE are set as follows: 𝜂= 0.1, 𝜆1 = 0.1, 𝜆2 = 0.01,
along with weight terms 𝑤1 = 1, 𝑤2 = 0.1, and 𝑤3 = 0.01. For
preference alignment, the coefficient 𝛼is set to 0.1. During infer-
ence, the decoding length 𝐿is the same as the slate size 𝑀= 5
using a beam search strategy with a width of 𝐵= 5, except in
specific ablation studies.
• Training and Deployment. For the offline evaluation, we uti-
lized a dataset collected from a commercial platform under Ten-
cent over a continuous one-month period. Specifically, we used
1 billion samples from all users for pre-training, selected 3% of
users for post-training, and reserved the last three days of data
(100 million samples) for testing. All training data consists of
filtered high-quality slate-level samples, where both the viewing
duration of individual items and the cumulative duration of the
entire slate exceed specific thresholds. For the online deploy-
ment, the system is supported by clusters equipped with tens of
NVIDIA H20 and L20 GPUs for training and serving respectively,
capable of handling hundreds of millions of daily requests.

C
Efficiency Experiments
For a fair comparison, we configure OneRec and HiGR with iden-
tical model settings and conduct all experiments under the same
hardware environment. Figure 4 plots inference efficiency (sam-
ples/minute) on the horizontal axis and effective view Recall@5
on the vertical axis. Without KV caching, our HiGR outperforms
OneRec-Beam (OneRec with beam search) by over 5% in perfor-
mance while achieving > 5× faster inference speed. It also signifi-
cantly outperforms OneRec-Greedy (OneRec with greedy search)
with faster inference speed. With KV caching, HiGR maintains
superior performance and inference efficiency.
D
Effects of Different Decoding Lengths
The shared fine-grained item generator within the Hierarchical
Slate Decoder enables HiGR to flexibly generate slates of arbi-
trary lengths. We validated this capability via a comparative study
against OneRec, evaluating performance across various slate sizes.
As shown in Figure 5, we conducted a comprehensive compari-
son between OneRec and HiGR by varying the slate length 𝐿∈
{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 10}. For the single-item generation scenario (𝐿= 1),
HiGR exhibits a substantial performance margin over OneRec. More
importantly, as the slate length increases, HiGR consistently retains
this superiority, demonstrating exceptional robustness and stability
for long-sequence generation compared to the SOTA baseline.
