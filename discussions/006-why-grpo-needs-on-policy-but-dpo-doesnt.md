# Why GRPO Needs On-Policy Candidates But DPO Doesn't

[English](006-why-grpo-needs-on-policy-but-dpo-doesnt.md) | [Chinese](006-why-grpo-needs-on-policy-but-dpo-doesnt.zh.md)

**Date**: 2026-04-28
**Context**: EXP-026~029 found that off-policy beam search resulted in GRPO clip=99%, R@500=2%; after switching on-policy, clip→92%, R@500=67.8%

---

## Core Discovery

EXP-028 proves a key conclusion: **reward repair (WeightedBehaviorReward, coverage ~100%) is not enough to activate the GRPO gradient**, and must be switched to on-policy beam search at the same time to allow the RL signal to be transmitted back.

This raises a question: RF-DPO (EXP-020) completely uses offline data and never requires on-policy sampling, and still pushes R@500 to 66.2%. Why doesn’t the DPO need an on-policy?

---

## Reasons why DPO does not require On-Policy

DPO loss:

```
L = -log σ(β · (log π_θ(y_w|x) - log π_ref(y_w|x)) - β · (log π_θ(y_l|x) - log π_ref(y_l|x)))
```

**The gradient direction is determined**: No matter what the current distribution of policy is, loss will always push up the relative log-prob of chosen and lower the relative log-prob of rejected. The annotation quality of offline preference pairs determines the optimization direction. The deviation between policy and data distribution does not affect the correctness of the gradient direction, but only affects the step size (implicitly controlled by β).

In addition, the β term of DPO is essentially the KL penalty `KL(π_θ || π_ref)`, which naturally constrains the policy not to deviate too far from ref - even if the data is offline, the distribution difference between the two is explicitly controlled.

---

## Reasons why GRPO must be On-Policy

GRPO advantage:

```
adv_i = (r_i - mean(r)) / std(r)    over group G candidates
```

PPO surrogate loss:

```
L = -min(ρ · adv, clip(ρ, 1-ε, 1+ε) · adv)
    where ρ = π_θ(y_i|x) / π_old(y_i|x)
```

The effectiveness of **advantage relies on candidates coming from the current policy distribution**. If candidates are generated by ref model (off-policy):

1. As training progresses, the policy and ref distributions deviate
2. importance ratio `ρ = π_θ/π_ref` deviates from 1, and a large number of samples fall on the clip boundary
3. clip=99%: Almost all gradients are cut off, and the advantage cannot be transmitted back no matter how accurate it is.
4. RL training exists in name only.

Evidence from EXP-028: reward coverage increased from 0.16% to ~100% (WeightedBehaviorReward), clip rate is still 99%, R@500 is still 2%. The reward signal is there, but the importance ratio is too biased and the gradient path is broken.

EXP-029 Switch on-policy beam: clip rate 99%→92%, R@500 2%→67.8%.

---

## Essential difference

| | DPO | GRPO |
|---|---|---|
| Gradient dependency | Fixed relative ordering of preference pairs | Advantage × importance ratio within group |
| Off-policy influence | Step size changes, Direction remains unchanged | Importance ratio deviates → clip fails → Gradient is 0 |
| KL constraints | β term implicit constraints to prevent drift | No explicit constraints, policy free drift |
| Data requirements | Offline preference pairs, once collected and valid forever | Each step requires policy candidates under the current distribution |

**Metaphor**: DPO is "give you two dishes and tell you which one is better" - offline data is completely sufficient. GRPO is "sample from your current menu and evaluate the relative quality" - if the menu is someone else's (ref model), the evaluation results are meaningless to you.

---

## Inference

- GRPO's on-policy requirements are **structural** and cannot be bypassed through reward shaping
- The offline feature of DPO is the **advantage**: preference data is collected once and can be used repeatedly, without the need for online sampling.
- It is reasonable to combine the two (first DPO and then GRPO): DPO uses offline data to push the model to a good starting point, and GRPO does online fine-tuning on this basis.
- The clip=99% of off-policy GRPO is a diagnostic indicator: once you see that the clip rate remains high, you first suspect the off-policy problem, not the reward problem.
